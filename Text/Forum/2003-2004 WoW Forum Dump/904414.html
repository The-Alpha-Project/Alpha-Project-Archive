|)ragonSword:

I like the idea of adjusting bag prices on the fly, but I fear that it falls into the category of "closing the barn door after the cows are gone." In other words, does this solve the problem at hand, or does it implement the solution too late to be useful?

With no item decay, bags are permanent. Players will therefore be affected by bag prices ONCE. Take the EverQuest example: they had a bag in EverQuest that could hold any 10 items or item stacks and render them weightless. ("Bag of the Tinkerers," as I recall.) To combat the possibility of players having too many of these bags, they placed the only vendor who sold these bags deep in a dungeon. Furthermore, they set the price RIDICULOUSLY high -- a lifetime of wealth for many players. The result? The highest level characters usually owned 6-8 of these bags eventually, and stored all their l33t gear in them for use on raids. Even the "prohibitively high" prices didn't solve the problem.

Why didn't it work? For a few reasons:

1. These bags directly increased the loot a character could carry. This made them a revenue-generating over time.
2. These bags never wore out, so their revenue stream was a permanent one.
3. Items never wore out either, so there was an ever-increasing need to hold more and more equipment.

If there is a "best bag" in WoW, it won't matter HOW expensive it is; the most effective characters WILL fill their slots with them for the benefit they provide.

Don't get me wrong, I still think automatic stabilizers in the economy are a fantastic idea. I just think that adjusting bag prices isn't the best solution. The bottom line is that it is the powergamers who will most need money sinks, but it is the casual gamers who will most suffer from this implementation, since the powergamers will already HAVE these top-quality bags.

Perhaps applying this same formula to high-level drop rates and high-level crafter ingredient prices (specifically for high-level consumables) would be more appropriate. Obviously, it would have to scale down, affecting lower-level drop rates and prices more minorly but still positively to avoid people from just going with the second-best mobs and second-best consumables, but the effect should trail off quickly so as not to stunt the developing characters in our attempts to slow the wealth acquisition rates of the powergamers.

Our goal (well, MY goal -- I shouldn't really speak for the rest) is to work through some serious concerns that are stumbling blocks to properly functioning economies in MMORPGs. This is a very touchy subject, since every MMORPG to date has had some problems with their economy -- sometimes minor, but usually MAJOR. While a broken functioning economy isn't the death knell for a video game, a strong economy makes the game work more smoothly. Thus, despite the level of boredom this thread (and all the others like it) may induce in some people, by exploring (and ideally solving) these problems before the game goes live, the game will actually wind up being MORE FUN. And ultimately, that's the whole goal of these threads, these boards, and the entire WoW development process.

As to the "bag formula" from above, you have to realize that it wasn't some rigorously derived mathematical proof or anything; it was just an illustrative example. Often, when dealing with complex numerical issues, a simple example in equation form can help illustrate your point far better than a few rambling paragraphs describing roughly how your idea would work.

Thanks for your thoughtful replies to my Bag Theory post (other than the one about me having no life.. heh). I hoped it would raise some interest amongst the economically minded on this forum. I want to respond to a few points raised. :)

First TehSuq, I must say I saw the comment about "closing the barn door after the cows are gone" coming. Yea I admit that at the time bag prices rise across the server player held item wealth will already be creeping up above the target, and people will already be carrying bags to put stuff in.

I still hold on to the usefulness of my theory though. The reason is that part of my post where I pointed out that I assume good bags, like good quality items, will be "soul bound". Apparently (from what Bliz has said so far) nothing is going to decay in this game. There will be consumable stuff, and there will be (quite a lot of) soul bound stuff.

If most good bags are soul bound there is not going to be a situation where players pass their bags down to their low level buddies when they upgrade, everyone is going to have to get their own (new) bags one way or another. Furthermore I see bags as equipment that players are going to want to upgrade fairly often as they become more wealthy.

There should be a more or less constant process of characters getting rid of old bags and replacing them with new better quality bags. Therefore by playing around with bag prices and the inflow of new bags into the player economy I still think we should be able to have some effect on player held item wealth in the short to medium term.

Here is an example. Players on a server find a way to get quite a lot of good quality items in a relatively short period of time (maybe an exploit). These items go into character inventories and the increase is noted by treasury. Players continue questing and MOB killing for a while, or whatever they were doing, then head back to town with the intention of storing their newly found item wealth.

When they get to town most of them have pretty full inventories and they badly need to offload some gear. Bank storage is already fairly squeezed, so players have a couple of choices. They can upgrade their bag system to store more stuff, or they can start selling (some old and some new) stuff to vendors to make more room for storage.

Players head off to the local bag shop, or a crafter they know who makes bags. Guess what? Bag prices have increased significantly! Who would have guessed? Some players are still going to choose to upgrade their bag storage system rather than selling stuff, but you can see that their motivation for selling stuff to NPC vendors instead of bagging it has now increased.
