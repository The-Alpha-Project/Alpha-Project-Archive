108. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/3/2003 11:56:13 PM GMTST
Q u o t e:
We are kind of at the point now where we realise that continuous flow of valuable items into the player economy without any way to get those items back to Treasury again could be a problem. So, please feel free to help us post possible solutions to this problem, ways to reduce the flow of rare items into the hands of players and ways to encourage players to give up items to the NPC sector of the economy.


In real life(tm), items flow back to the "treasury", so-to-speak, when newer, better items are introduced into the system. Perhaps the best way to remove items from the system is to promote transition to newer, more worthwhile goods on a constant basis. Thus, hoarding Item X isn't as useful if Item X2 comes out which makes Item X less valuable.

In WoW, this could translate to:

1. Revamping of trade skill recipies on a constant (say, monthly) basis, replacing old, unused ones, with new ones that may have slight advantages (better rewards) over something that is being heavily produced at this time, devaluating, and later absorbing, the created goods.

2. Similarly, with the Questing System: quests that are heavily replayed remain viable, but quests that are no longer done due to "weak" rewards get revamped to produce a reward slightly better than the ones that are heavily replayed. People have to find the new ones, instead of going through the same old ones. Meanwhile, items/rewards from obsolete quests are absorbed back into the economy and those items aren't produced anymore, eventually dying off somehow (deleted chars, sold to vendors, etc.)

3. General Monster drops - If players aren't killing monster X because he doesn't offer anything good anymore, change his drops to something that might be more valuable.

This constant cycle is well demonstrated in reality: DELL doesn't sell 286's anymore, because you can buy P4s. There's no limit to how many P4's you can buy, but people won't hoard because they know the P5 is right around the corner.

Granted, this can't be fixed for every item. In reality, milk is milk, generally can't be "improved" upon, and is considered a necessity, just as there are probably core items in WoW that will remain the same throughout the game's lifetime.

Thoughts?

112. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/4/2003 1:41:03 AM GMTST
Q u o t e:
Scree, the most deadly flaw in your post is that you can only improve upon equipment for so long. If you keep inching it up over time, your character won't matter at all, only his equipment will matter. I don't think anybody wants to keep making the high end gear into "even higher end" sort of stuff. Well, Everquest is the best example of this actually in motion, except that in EQ, old items aren't removed from the system- you can still play the first quest from the original EQ released nearly 5 years ago. This ends up creating graveyards in the old content as expansions are released and content is added. No one wants to go to an older zone because the rewards are obsolete.

I think the solution, and danger, is all about pacing. If you introduce new items too quickly, you run the risk of trivializing the reward for a quest. If you move too slowly, someone inevitably "figures out" the best items for every class at a given level, posts it all over the web, and people won't want anything else.

There is no way that every item will be useful to someone at a given time. There are always "optimal" items (and, of course, skills and stats) which are generally a subset of all given goods in the game at a time. Human nature is to find and use the optimal items (call it competitive spirit), and part of the MMORPG appeal is to find the next one.

With a system that allows recycling of discarded goods, combined with a well-designed replacement/enhancement system, you can create an endlessly fresh experience without resorting to expansion packs. The hard part is, of course, making sure that the reward system is dynamic enough to support constant enhancement and progression.

The currency issue is similarly solved if NPC vendors are stocked in the same way. Goods sold are updated to better resemble the current demand, and older goods are phased out as demand dies. This also opens up the possibility of older items being valued within the player economy as "limited edition" commodities that aren't produced by the world anymore, raising their inherent value.

The fact that an MMORPG is a constantly evolving world is unquestionable. Unfortunately, today's MMORPGs are designed like single player games: to be beaten and put back on the shelf. Expansion packs and timely content updates solve one end of the problem, but the other end- the graveyards of monsters, items, quests- still hasn't been addressed.

120. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/4/2003 3:00:53 AM GMTST
Woa. Hold it. Let's back up here. I would like to adress the rogue pickpocketing thing first. Here are my thoughts on the whole thing. Firstly, it was a good idea on Blizzard's end to have a separate loot system for pickpocketing. The major advantage to this is that rogues won't be stealing from the economy directly. What does that mean? This means that there is a separate section for items that can be stolen. This separate category will still be included in calculating how much of everything there is on a server. Now, as for items that can be stolen, I think there should be multiple groups of items organized depending on the stregnth/level of the items. For example, group one there would be: Noobie potions, noobie swords, noobie maces, etc. Also, the amount of gold that can be stolen should be limited to lets say 10 gold for group one. In group two, possible items that can be stolen would be like: intermediate potions, intermediate swords, intermediate maces, and a possible 25 gold. Group three: Uber potions, uber swords, uber maces, and up to 50 gold. Now these gold numbers are just arbitrary and the kinds of items that can be stolen wouldn't be limited to just swords, maces, or potions. Depending on who you steal from, the items that can be stolen will change. Finally, how would you steal from group one or group 2? The answer is quite simple. Your current proficiency level for pickpocketing will determine what group of items you can steal from. For example: Joey the rogue is very skilled in pickpocketing, therefore he can steal from higher groups. Bjorn is not skilled in pickpocketing so he would steal from lower groups. Get the idea? Now, onto the item and gold flow!

Here is my idea on how stolen gold and stolen items would flow in and out of the economy and in and out of player/NPC inventories. First off, the amount of gold that could potentially be stolen would be fixed to some number and the amounts of different kinds of items that can be stolen would be fixed as well. Second, we have to understand a simple concept. Has anyone heard of an isotope in science? If not, isotopes are simply different "versions" of elements. For example, let's say that you have an atom of Hydrogen with 0 neutrons. An isotope of a Hydrogen atom would be just like normal Hydrogen but with lets say 3 neutrons instead. What the hell does this have to do with the topic at hand?!?!? Here's how it relates. All items that are grouped in a potentially stolen loot category would simply be isotopes of the regular items. This means that a stolen Elixir of Lion's strength would be exactly the same as the non-stolen version, except for one thing. Instead of going through the treasury and any NPC inventory and all the other stuff that a normal item flows through, the stolen version of an item would cycle through player inventories but never actually go through the rest of the economy. For example, Bob the rogue has a current proficiency level of 1. Bob steals an item from an NPC. This item is randomly drawn from group 1 of the stolen item pools. Bob is very unlucky and gets one silverleaf. So, Bob decides to trade this silverleaf with another player for gold. Bob gets 1 gold and is sad. The new owner of the silverleaf says: HA! What a retard. I'm going to sell this item to an NPC vendor for this thing's real value! The player sells the silverleaf for 10 gold. The player receives his gold, but the silverleaf does not go back to the NPC vendor who bought it. Oh no, not at all! Recall that the stolen silverleaf is an isotope of the real thing so it therefore goes back to the stolen loot pool inventory!!!!!! So, to sum it all up, if a stolen item is sold, it simply goes back to the pool it was stolen from. If it is not sold, then it stays in a player's inventory. Simple as that! When it comes to someone stealing gold and then buying something with it, the stolen (isotope) gold just goes back to stolen loot pool from which it was taken.

Here are some simple concepts to help you understand this all:

Amount of stolen gold + Amount of real gold = Total gold on server

Amount of stolen item X + Amount of real item X = Total item X on server

Amount of stolen gold in player inventories + Amount of stolen gold in loot pools = Total amount of stolen gold

Amount of stolen item X in player inventories + Amount of stolen item X in loot pools = Total amount of stolen item X

These concepts are all very basic. But I think the whole general idea of stolen items and gold flowing through player inventories and loot pools mentioned above would work well. What do you guys think of my idea? Could it work? Tell me what you think. :D
