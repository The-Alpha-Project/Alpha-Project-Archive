69. Re: Do You Read? | 1/22/2004 2:22:18 PM GMTST
I read big time. And if any of you teenagers (earlier ones) don't, I suggest you very quickly begin to. Nothing will make your college English classes easier than having read a ton already. Even in middle/high school I was always grades ahead in English because I read all the time. I enjoy breezing through classes.

Anyway, I have a special bookcase for my favorites:

David Eddings' series. Even the lesser known ones.

All David Gemmell books, but start with Legend and then move on to the coolest character ever, Tenaka Khan, in The King Beyond the Gate. Honestly, I used to rank Eddings as my favorite author; now, Gemmell has taken his spot.

Robin Hobb's Assassin books (cheers to you, Kat). I've always related to Fitz, and, in Hobb's supposed last book starring him, the "action" stops almost two thirds of the way into the book, and the rest is devoted simply to the characters. And that was wonderful.

Dennis L. McKiernan's The Iron Tower trilogy and the Silver Call duology.

The first four or five Robert Jordan books. Still, I despise the women in those books, and the way the men are forced to react to them. If anyone's read those books, you know of Lan. Well, doesn't he have a great history? The sword placed in his hand...and what is his destiny/greatest ambition? To marry and be a certified slave to a #@%$!. Rand being unable/unwilling to kill the women who constantly are attempting to kill him, as well, begins to grate. Mine is a definite love/hate relationship with this series. It can be awesome, and it can be mind-numbingly bad.

Drizzt's books, but especially those in the Drow city. I love that place.

L. E. Modessit Jr.'s Recluce books.

Chris Bunch's The Seer King, The Demon King and The Warrior King. This has explicit sex, fairly frequently, so be, um, warned. But his characters are some of the best ever, second only in power to Gemmell's. There's one scene in particular, involving people waiting to be executed, that is one of the most emotional I've ever read.

Many of the Dragonlance books, but especially those focusing on the Majere brothers and Raistlin's story.

Alan Dean Foster's Flinx books.

Herbert's Dune series. I don't recommend this series for the casual reader; it can get pretty heavy.

Edit: I forgot the Harry Potter series. Thanks to whomever mentioned it; don't dismiss this series, it is not just for kids. Also, good to see other Gemmell lovers. Oh, and a Lovecraft or two, too. The Rats in the Walls is a great short story of his. Speaking of shorts, if anyone's seen the movie The Thing, the short story it's based upon is far better: "Who Goes There" by John W. Campbell Jr. Actually, I took an Intro to Sci-Fi course once, and the entire book, "Science Fiction: The Science Fiction Research Association Anthology," by Patricia S. Warrick, et al, is a great read.

Okay, read any of the above and you'll have a good time.
