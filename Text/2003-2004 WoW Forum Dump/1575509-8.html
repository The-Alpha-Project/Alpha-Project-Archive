He's saying there is no number which fits your description... formally, let a and b be real numbers less than 1; for any a there exists b such that a < b < 1. So for any real number less than 1, there's another real number between it and 1. (The same actually applies to rational numbers.)

0.9~ is defined by a geometric series. 9 * Sum k=1 to inf 1/(10^k)

Since |1/10| < 1, this geometric series is convergent.

Any geometric series in the form a_k = x^k
for |x| < 1 can be solved for by:

Sum k=0 to inf of a_k = 1 / (1-x)

If we plug in 1/10 = x you get 1 / (9/10) = 10/9

Multiply the result by 9 to get 10, but since we only want the sum from 1 to infinity, not 0 to infinity, we have to subtract by 9 * a_0, which is 9 * 1/(10^0) = 9, so we get 1.

Now that this is proved, for those who didn't believe it, here's another math fact for you to argue about.

Take the series defined by
1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4 + 1/5 - 1/6 + ...

This series converges to ln(2)
Now take the series defined by
1 + 1/3 - 1/2 + 1/5 + 1/7 - 1/4 + ...

This series has the every term in it that the first does, but it converges to (3/2)ln(2)

Infact you can take this series and rearrange the order in which you add the terms to have the series converge to any real number, and you can also rearrange the terms to have the series diverge.

Q u o t e:
1 - countable set
0.999~ - uncountably infinite

Wrong. .9~ is rational (it equals 9/9 = 1; this would be circular except that we can prove .9~ = 1 by other means), and the rational numbers are a countably infinite set. (And, believe me, I do NOT want to be dragged into the discussion of why rationals are countably infinite and real numbers are not.)

The mistake aydam makes is that he jumps from (essentially) the set of integers shifted over some finite number of decimal places (for which the completeness property does not hold) to the set of real numbers (for which the completeness property does hold).

In other words, the stipulation "to X decimal places" essentially means you're taking the set of integers and dividing it by 10^X. The completeness property doesn't hold for integers (there are no integers between 1 and 2, but they are not equal). The stipulation "to an infinite number of decimal places" essentially means any real number. The completeness property holds for real numbers, so your analogy doesn't hold.
