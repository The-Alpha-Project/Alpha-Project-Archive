141. Re: WoW Economic Management Challenges | 12/6/2003 7:34:29 AM GMTST
After reading the posts in this thread (in which my head was swimming in equations), I came to realize... you guys have way to much time on your hands. But alas, you are right in your calculations. However, you have overlooked a few things. One such thing is that as an Multiplayer Online Game, you cannot have limiations, nor can you have a system that is mainly based on one particular economic solution.

I would like to propose my own view to look at. It's not an monetary equation, but more of an observation on how the economy would operate in a typical fashion. Yes, I know there will be several people who will disagree with what I am about to write.

First off, take off the ideas of a Treasury, a limited number of items, etc.
Money and items (equipment, quest items, other loot) are not limited in how much can exist at one exact time. The number of items on a particular server can be endless. That gets rid of the worries of someone cornering a particular market based on particular item.
Next, lets look at vendors. Vendors themselves have the ability to sell a pre-generated list of items. This list cannot change, and will contain some form of normal items that are used by players as equipment or consumed itesms. Thus you have one vendor who would sell swords as his pre-generated list type, and another who would sell gems as his pre-generated list type. Next, add in the ability for each vendor to accept (buy) items from players. When the vendor buys this item, it is added to another list on the vendor, we will call it the Temporary Stock. It is called temporary because each time the server is reset or brought down for maintainance, all information about this stock is reduced to zero (which effectively takes out items in the economy). Now the key thing about vendors is that they sell low-grade items in terms of equipment (the more valuable armors and weapons would be obtained through other means), however, they would be the primary source of various other necessities. This could come in the guise of BASE items required for tradeskills (such as a vial, a spit, a flask, or a bowl) or the guise of REAGENTS (in which are instantly consumed when the spell is successfully cast). Add to the fact that there would be different types of consumables (you would have higher grade/quality items for higher skills and spells) and that you would need to buy some of these in bulk. Since these items would mainly be supplied by vendors, it would make these items extremely hard to find in other means. However, what about the other items, such as armor and weapons (and various other equipment).
Well, these items would serve as the basis of the player-run portion of the economy. Lets start off be adding the fact that not all creatures will drop some type of loot. Thus, there is already an edge on the amount of items coming into the world (as opposed to a game like Diablo 2). Next, lets add the fact that there will not always be items useful to a particular character, and not everyone will horde items just to sell them to another player. So many of those items would go to vendors (into their Temporary Stock) while provide the player with meager cash (adding a miniscule portion of money into the economy). However, some creatures will drop weapons or armor. Depending on the quality of the said armor or weapons (which can range from store-bought, sub-store-bought, and above store-bought) would determine the economic value to players. A low grade items that is less in effectiveness than, say, the lowest sword availible in the vendor's inventory would automatically be considered as vendor loot (in which the player sells, adding to the Temporary Stock again, and recieves a small portion of money) or intended to rot on the corpse of the monster. The average weapons could be looted and sold as well, but they would most likely stay on a corpse to rot as well. While the above standard items (mainly the magical ones and player-created items) would have a different role.
The key thing to realize in a player-run system is that not everything is desireable. There are some things people want more than others. That is also limited to what people can afford. I will set up an example.

There are four total players:
Bob, who is selling a Keen Longsword (he is asking for 100 gold).
John, who is looking to buy a better sword (but only has 75 gold), but has an Enhanced Club (which he wants to sell for 25 gold).
Jane, who has 25 gold and wants to buy a better weapon.
Davis, who has a Shortsword of Might and a Keen Dagger (which he is selling for 75 gold and 15 gold, respectively). However, he has 150 gold in his pocket, and himself is looking for a better weapon.

Bob auctions off his sword for 100 gold. John, being the first to see that Bob is selling a weapon he wants realizes he only has 75 gold, but if he sells his club, he can get the sword. So he auctions off his club. About this time, Jane comes up and sees that John is selling his club (a better weapon than the one she has currently), however, she doesn't want a blunt weapon (either by choice, or by class restriction, etc.). However, Davis shows up and see's Bob's auction and buys the sword. Davis buys the sword, and puts up his newly-replaced sword for sale. John sees this new sword up for sale and sees that it is 75 gold, the same amount that he has, so John goes to buy that sword instead. However, he wants to buy a meal or two for health, so he still wants to sell his club. Davis, seeing as he had no trouble selling his current item, decides to sell the dagger he had in his backpack. Jane sees the dagger, which she can use, and chooses to buy it. Everyone, except John, is satisfied with their transactions and they decide to leave. However, John realizes after awhile that his club isn't selling, so he decides to leave.

So, in the end results:
Bob sold his sword and made 100 gold.
John didn't sell his club, but still bought a sword that he could use, even though it isn't what he wanted.
Jane bought her new dagger, and still has 10 gold that she can spend or save up.
Davis, being the excellent businessman he is, sold both of his weapons, and obtained a new weapon, and has 140 gold.

What does this prove? Player economies are set up in a way that not everything will be dependent on the most typical item that everyone will want, especially at one time. However, this line of thinking changes when we stop focusing on a small player group, and look at a larger player group.

Lets say a particular monster drops two weapons: a Longsword of Swift Justice, and a Dagger of Maiming. The items that drops from this weapon do not both drop at the same time, and in all factuallity, the Longsword of Swift Justice is a fairly rare item that drops. Say, his chances of dropping the item are 1 in 100. However, the Dagger of Maiming drops 74 of those 99 times that Longsword does not drop, and there are 25 kills in which nothing drops at all. To top it off, the chances of the weapon dropping reset each time the monster respawns. This is where we enter a state of over-supply and demand.
It seems that the Longsword of Swift Justice is all the rage, and that many people want one. So, people start killing the monsters that are supposed to drop them. In the begininng, the Longsword of Swift Justice already will cost a large ammount. Lets say 500 gold. The Dagger of Maiming, which isn't that bad a weapon itself, opens the market price around 300 gold. Now, it may not be on the same level as the sword, but a lot of people are buying the dagger. As a result that the dagger is wanted and fairly easy to obtain, the dagger's price will actually go UP. However, as time goes on, more and more daggers are entering the equation. Eventually, the market for the dagger goes down. So, we start seeing the price of the dagger dropping to as low as 50 gold.
But, what about the Sword? Well, lets say that there are 6000 people playing on one server, and that 3000 of them are wanting the Longsword of Maiming. However, at the current rate that they appear in the economy, they are quite rare. So, while the dagger is lowering in price, the sword itself is raising in price, not solely because people are wanting to make a profit, but because the rate of the sword entering the economy is so stagnant, that the player demand forces the price up. It's reasons like this that make a player economy hard, but viable (and this example holds true to many real-world applications, but not for the length of period in a game).
However, the key to all of it, is that while some items are more desired than others, not all of them are going to be predominate than the others. There are exceptions to this.
What is an exception? Well, lets take an example from Everquest. The majority of all higher-end items are generally no-drop. What does this do to the high-end items that are not no-drop? Their price increases in range because their are a desired commodity in he community that are viable sources of equipment.

Now, this system is not without it's flaws. In fact, my arguement is not as strong as the ones from American_Might or the rest. The idea of having a limitless economy is hard to work around, but the idea is to curb it from going out of hand. However, this is a system that has proven to work, as strange as it might sound.
In any place, you will have those people who hoard cash and items. And while that might seem like a viable option to compensate for the imbalance, that compensation is an imbalance in itself. Not because it balances everything, but because it is limiting what people can do. And it makes it easier for someone to corner a market.

If you find anything in my post that doesn't make sense to you, just ask and I will try to clarify it.

Wow, ton of great posts, guys. I'm impressed with the understanding of economics most of you have shown. One major thing I believe you are all overlooking is the danger of controlled cash and item flows within a game. Let's take a look at the successes and failures of games like DAoC that have fairly dynamic currency systems combined with restriction of currency generation. First, setting any specific amount of gold for one NPC is a good way to guarantee that NPC, and likely the village/outpost/town he is located in will become obsolete as times passes. The sheer amount of gold the average merchant in a city like Camelot in DAoC dishes out in terms of loot bought from characters each day is staggering. Likewise, the only merchants who receive siginificant amounts of gold daily are those who sell specific supplies, especially crafting supplies. The basis of the fairly successful DAoC carfting economy is the fact that crafting is a staggering expense. To increase my characters crafting level to it's highest possible status, I relied not only on my own quests for coin, but also on the collective work of my entire guild, many of whom had higher level characters and spent quite a large amount of time either adventuring for gold to give me or plying their own crafts to generate funds for the guild. What matters is not the total amount of gold within a server, but rather the amount of money burned by players creating and using items. To successfully create an economy that won't become flooded by currency, all that is required is creation of somewhat necessary sources of money burn. Horse and griffon rides, rent, reagents and ingredients in recipes, etc... money burn off associated with any given item being tied to the generation of that item and the item's power level. Creating a mighty sword might require considerable investment by a smith. The smith invests 500 "gold" as well as several hours of his time questing for ultra rare ingredients. When he completes the sword he sells it for 700 "gold" to a warrior who has generated money by both crafting lesser items and adventuring. Only the gold wich was gained specifically by the warrior from adventuring has been added to the economy. At the same time the 500 gold worth of ingredients has been burned off by giving it to an NPC vendor.
The problem with comparing games like Diablo with an MMORPG is that Diablo IS NOT an MMORPG. It's a multiplayer online game, yes, however the lack of a constant world in which the players interact change the nature of the economy dramatically. At any given time in Diablo there could be 25,000+ people doing Pindleskin runs to generate gobs of ultra high level items. A similar creature in WoW will only be campable by a small group, probably no more than the amount of people in one adventuring group. This limits the influx of gold from that creature into each server. Because players are unable to then leave that server and take their items/currency to another server, farming a specific creature becomes vastly less viable as a source of currency/item generation. In that sense Diablo and Diablo II are so unlike an MMORPG that their economies cannot be used as a viable economic model when being compared to MMORPG economies.
By severely restricting influx of currency/items into a server, Blizzard can avoid almost all of your proposed concerns without any of the vaunted formulae you've used. An economy in which only adventurers partake and in which the number of different goods being sold is miniscule by comparison to a real world economy is almost laughably simplistic. The only concern for Blizzard is to create viable sources of money burn off, as mentioned above, so that the amount of gold in the player economy doesn't become ridiculous.
As for sources of money burn other than NPC bought gear, potions, etc..consider player vanity as well as advanced item decay. The beauty of games like DAoC is that, although my gear is probably the best I can get for my character as his level, it is also player made and player spellcrafted. It also decays. So every month or so I am commissioning and replacing one or two parts of my gear. I'm able to maintain the level of use because there are a number of powerful spellcrafters on my server who can easily enchant the exact same thing I had before. The difference, of course, is that I am always using a portion of my money to buy new equipment. Say I pay 500 "gold" for an armband. After several months that armband decays from constant repair and abuse (damn trolls drooling on my armor again...). I pay another 500 gold to get the same gear back. So I go to my friend the crafter and his partner the spellcrafter. I give them the gold and they make my armband. The crafter pays 150 gold in materials, or spends gold on adventuring to get the materials, and receives 200 of the 500 gold. Next, the spellcrafter spends 250 gold on materials, and receives 300 of the 500 gold. So now both the crafter and spell crafter are 50 gold richer. I am 500 gold poorer. The net loss of 400 gold within the PLAYER economy has gone to materials, or the cost of griffin rides, healing potions, and repair of gear incurred by the crafters in their quests to gain materials in order to craft for me.
So simply by adventuring for loot I cause a certain amount of gold to be burned off of the economy, because the cost of adventuring includes not only NPC direct costs such as griffin rides, any kind of ressurection sickness type healing, and basic repairs if i can't do that myself, but also NPC indirect costs because the people who I need to craft my gear have to burn their own gold to craft/adventure. In the long run the cost of item decay burns off a massive amount of gold. There is no need to keep a specific amount of gold with NPCs because they are not, in truth, part of the economy, and to add to it, if they run out of gold, and their surrounding merchants run out of gold, their locales become obsolete and less player populated. This creates a whole slew of problems for players and Blizzard alike.
Now for item limits. As it has been pointed out, item limits would encourage hoarding and monopolistic prices. Imagine, not only will people be selling Gold on ebay, but basic reagents for healing potions. If a single powerful guild decides to do so, its members could easily concentrate on cornering the market of one reagent from nearly every recipe in the game. Imagine, several years into the game, your character no longer being playable because the prestigous guild on your server has nearly wiped out the availability of a large number of important ingredients and will only sell them to certain people or for outrageous prices. In this item caps become very dangerous. At the same time limiting player inventories or the amount of a specific item a player can have will lead not only to lag caused by constant logging of mules and mains, but also to an economy in which player run stores and consignment merchants become difficult to produce. Limiting palyer inventory in games like Diablo, where infinite instancing means sickening masses of items per player, is perfectly logical. In smaller worlds where instancing is rare, such as an MMORPG server, it just creates muling and other such annoyances and leads to less flavor for people who want to role play a merchant.
Well, it looks like my post has gotten ridiculously long. I'll post more later, let me know what you all think.

The whole idea of having "loot pools" (such as NPC strongholds) is to act as a limitation or safety mechanism on the distrubution of wealth, so the economy doesn't get out of control.

Without limited "loot pools" that rewards are drawn out of mobs and NPCs could distribute huge amounts of wealth very quickly when players find ways to exploit sections of the economy, before Bliz finds out and can do anything about it. The idea is the existance of "loot pools" will be pretty much invisible to players most of the time, unless something goes wrong.

It's only when something weird happens and wealth starts flooding out of a particular loot pool at a faster than expected (budgeted) rate that loot pool limitations will come into play and tighten wealth distribution from that activity automatically. This is why we shouldn't allocate too much loot for distrubution through quest rewards, or arena combat or factional PvP rewards or whatever.

Regarding your second point, I'm thinking that loot pools will probably hold a certain amount of currency and a certain "value of items" (rather than actual items). Then when items are distributed from a loot pool the item hidden value will be deducted from the total "value of items" held in the pool. So it won't matter if most players choose one reward or another, the pool won't run out of that item.
